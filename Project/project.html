The Economics of Survival in Men’s Professional Tennis

1. Introduction

1.1. Aims

This project examines the distribution of prize money in men’s professional tennis and assesses how many players can realistically afford to make a living from the sport. To do so, it combines a newly constructed panel dataset of ATP prize money with inflation-adjusted cost estimates from Balliauw et al. (2017), quantifying income inequality in the men’s circuit and approximating the ranking threshold at which players achieve financial break-even (Schoettl et al., 2025).

1.2. Building an ATP Prize Money Panel Dataset

This project contributes a new panel dataset covering all ATP-ranked players active between the 2014 and 2025 seasons. The dataset records prize money earned across all tournaments, and the code is designed to be easily extended to earlier players or future seasons by expanding the date range. A key challenge was accessing the ATP website’s underlying API endpoints, as the platform does not allow straightforward data extraction using standard Python scraping libraries. An unresolved challenge is finding a way to iterate over a longer time span, as the website blocks access after extracting data for approximately eleven years. In addition to this dataset, a separate file containing ATP player characteristics was constructed using this code. To enable comparisons with country-level income inequality, Gini coefficient data were retrieved from the World Bank API. Inflation adjustments were performed using data from the Federal Reserve Bank API, which requires user authentication and an access key, while exchange rate data were obtained from the European Central Bank.

2. Tennis is a very unequal sport

Tennis is the fifth most-watched sport worldwide, after Football, Basketball, Cricket, and Baseball, which allows it to rank as the eighth highest-revenue-generating sport annually. Despite this, the distribution of income among players is by far the most unequal when considering the top 30 earners, compared with Ice Hockey, American Football, Basketball, and Baseball.

When considering all ranked players, the Gini coefficient of men’s professional tennis reaches 0.924 in 2025, indicating an extremely high level of income inequality, close to the upper bound of the index. To put this magnitude into perspective, the first chart compares professional tennis with countries that are among the most unequal and among the most equal income distributions observed internationally in the same year, such as Colombia and Norway, respectively. The comparison highlights that income inequality in professional tennis is substantially higher than that observed even in highly unequal countries' income distributions.

Across years, the ATP Tour’s Gini coefficient shows little variation when all ranked players are considered, indicating relatively stable prize money inequality over time. However, the time-series chart below reveals two key insights that motivate an analysis of the financial break-even point: inequality reaches levels comparable to Colombia only when the sample is restricted to the top 200 players, while among the top 50 it is lower than in the United States; and when focusing on the top 300 players, the Gini trend becomes more negative between 2014 and 2025, suggesting a decline in inequality that may reflect a growing share of players reaching their financial break-even point.

2. Are more players achieving financial equilibrium?

To better understand the data, the distribution of prize money is examined first. Because prize money is highly concentrated at low values, a density plot obscures the financial break-even point; a line chart is therefore used to clearly identify the threshold at which players begin to finance their professional careers through prize money.

According to Balliauw et al. (2017), the average annual cost of competing on the professional tennis circuit is USD 113,494 in 2025, after adjusting for inflation and exchange rates. Therefore, professional tennis becomes financially sustainable from world No. 274 onward, where this player achieves a marginally positive balance of USD 319. In other words, only 274 out of more than 2,000 professional players fully finance their season through prize money. Compared with 2014, the break-even threshold has shifted downward by more than 80 ranking positions, indicating a modest but meaningful improvement in the financial sustainability of professional tennis over the past decade.

The next time-series chart shows a gradual improvement in the share of professional tennis players able to fully finance their season through prize money: in 2014, about 7% of players reached the financial break-even point, rising by 3.7 percentage points to 10.7% in 2025. Overall, the trend indicates a modest but meaningful expansion in the group of players reaching break-even, consistent with the downward shift in the break-even ranking observed in earlier figures; however, the limited magnitude of this change suggests that structural constraints in the professional tennis income model persist.

3. The Predictor Power of Prize Money on Longevity

Motivated by the fact that only a small share of professional tennis players can fully finance their careers through prize money, this analysis examines whether cumulative prize earnings predict career duration among retired players.

Using an inflation-adjusted panel of ATP prize money (constant 2025 USD), retired players are defined as those inactive in 2025. An initial inspection of the data reveals two extreme outliers—Roger Federer and Rafael Nadal. Even after excluding them, a small group of players continues to accumulate prize money far above the rest. Accordingly, the analysis restricts the sample to players earning no more than USD 10 million, focusing on careers closer to the financial sustainability threshold.

A Random Forest regression model estimated in scikit-learn shows a positive relationship between cumulative earnings and career length; however, despite a relatively high R², substantial dispersion, particularly for short careers, suggests that earnings alone do not determine longevity, highlighting the role of additional factors such as injuries, sponsorships, and institutional support.

4. Conclusion and Discusion

This project highlights the extreme income inequality in men’s professional tennis and the very small share of players able to finance a full competitive season through prize money, reinforcing recent findings in the literature (Balliauw et al., 2017; Schoettl et al., 2025). Its main contribution is the introduction of a temporal perspective, allowing the evolution of the financial break-even point to be analysed over time. The newly constructed panel dataset and publicly available code support regular updates and extensions, encouraging further research on policies to improve the financial sustainability of professional tennis careers. A remaining limitation is that annual costs were adjusted for inflation, while actual costs may have increased faster, potentially overstating the share of players reaching break-even—an issue future work could address by automating and updating cost estimates.
